The No Surprises Act

Signed December 27, 2020 • Effective January 1, 2022 • Pub. L. 116-260

For an 8-Year-Old:

Imagine you go to a restaurant. The menu says a burger is $5. But when the check comes, they charge you $50 because the cook wasn't "in your plan." That's not fair, right? The No Surprises Act is like a rule that says restaurants (hospitals) have to tell you the real price AND can't surprise you with a huge bill from a cook (doctor) you didn't pick.

Three Big Protections

1. No Surprise Bills

Ban on surprise billing for emergency services and non-emergency services at in-network facilities.

2. Cost Protection for Uninsured

If you don't have insurance, you get a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) before your visit, and can dispute bills that are way more than the estimate.

3. Price Transparency

Hospitals and doctors must be upfront about costs. No hiding fees or making it hard to find out what things cost.

Federal IDR vs. PPDR — What's the Difference?

Federal IDR

Independent Dispute Resolution

  • Between providers and health plans
  • For insured patients
  • 45 CFR §§ 149.510–149.520
  • ~1.19M disputes in H1 2025
  • Provider win rate: ~88%

PPDR (Auxilium's Focus)

Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution

  • Between patients and providers
  • For uninsured/self-pay patients
  • 45 CFR § 149.620
  • Volumes not publicly tracked by CMS
  • Patient-favorable structure

Five Requirements to File a PPDR Dispute

1
Uninsured or Self-Pay

No group plan, individual coverage, Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, VA, or IHS. Self-pay patients who told the provider before care qualify too.

2
Care After January 1, 2022

Services must have been provided on or after the NSA effective date.

3
Good Faith Estimate Received

You must have received a GFE. No GFE = no PPDR (but you can file a CMS complaint).

4
$400+ Over Estimate

Billed charges exceed the GFE by at least $400 per provider/facility. Calculated independently, NOT added together.

5
Within 120 Days

Must file within 120 calendar days of receiving the initial bill (not the date of service).

Anti-Retaliation Protection

Providers cannot send disputed bills to collections, threaten collections, or charge late fees during the PPDR process. Penalty: $100/day per violation (42 U.S.C. § 300gg-22).

PPDR Process Flow

Step-by-step: How a patient dispute actually works

For an 8-Year-Old:

It's like returning something at a store. You fill out a form, pay a tiny fee ($25), a fair helper looks at both sides, and then decides what you really owe. If you win, you even get your $25 back!

Day 0

1. File the Dispute

Submit form CMS-10791 via the Federal IDR Portal, electronically, or by mail to C2C Innovative Solutions (P.O. Box 45105, Jacksonville, FL 32232).

Upon Request

2. Pay $25 Admin Fee

Pay via credit card, PayPal, Venmo, or money order. If you win, this $25 gets deducted from what you owe.

Days 1-5

3. SDR Entity Assigned

HHS selects a Selected Dispute Resolution (SDR) entity (currently C2C Innovative Solutions). They check if your case qualifies, with a 21-business-day window to fix any problems.

+3 Business Days

4. Conflict Check

The SDR entity checks for conflicts of interest. If conflicted, HHS assigns a different SDR entity.

10 Business Days

5. Provider Responds

The provider submits credible information showing: (a) charges reflect medically necessary service AND (b) charges are based on unforeseen circumstances.

During Step 5

6. Settlement Window

Provider may offer a settlement. Any settlement must reduce what you owe by at least $12.50 (50% of $25 fee).

30 Business Days

7. Final Decision

The SDR entity makes a binding determination. No appeal allowed. If provider fails to prove their case on GFE items, you pay GFE amount. On non-GFE items, you pay $0.

What Happens After the Decision?

If Patient Wins

  • Provider adjusts bill within 30 days
  • $25 fee deducted from amount owed
  • Monitor for unauthorized credit reporting
  • If provider doesn't adjust: CMS complaint

If Provider Wins

  • Patient owes lesser of billed charge or median network rate (GFE items)
  • Patient owes billed charge (non-GFE items)
  • $25 fee NOT refunded
  • No appeal exists — but can negotiate directly

AI & Healthcare Laws

Key laws and regulations shaping AI in patient-provider dispute resolution

For an 8-Year-Old:

When computers help decide things about your health or money, there have to be rules to keep things fair. Just like how a video game needs rules so nobody cheats, AI in healthcare needs rules so the computer doesn't make mistakes that hurt people.

FTC Health Breach Notification Rule (16 CFR Part 318)
HIGH RELEVANCE

Covers vendors of personal health records. If Auxilium's database is breached, it must notify affected patients within 60 days.

Penalty: Up to $50,120 per incident

Why it matters for Auxilium: Even though HIPAA likely doesn't directly apply (Auxilium isn't a hospital or insurer), the FTC rule catches health-data companies in its net. This is the primary federal privacy law governing Auxilium.

FTC Act Section 5 — Unfair or Deceptive Practices
HIGH RELEVANCE

The FTC can take action against any company using AI in ways that are unfair or deceptive. This is why Auxilium must:

  • Disclose all fees prominently
  • Tell patients they can self-file for just $25
  • Never claim the AI gives "legal advice"
  • Never predict dispute outcomes

Key precedent: FTC fined DoNotPay $193,000 in 2024 for misleading AI-lawyer claims.

Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL) Doctrine
HIGH SEVERITY

Filing documents with a federal administrative body is a hallmark of legal practice. AI tools that cross from document preparation into legal judgment face serious risk.

The TIKD Rule

In Florida Bar v. TIKD Services (2021), a tech company that helped people contest traffic tickets was permanently shut down for UPL — even though no consumers were harmed. This is why Auxilium must stay on the "tool" side of the line.

Safe zone: Document prep, form population, deadline tracking

Danger zone: Legal advice, filing recommendations, success predictions, strategy

HIPAA & the AI Privacy Gap
NUANCED

Here's the twist: HIPAA likely does NOT apply to Auxilium because it's neither a covered entity nor a business associate. When a patient voluntarily shares bills, HIPAA doesn't follow the data.

But this creates a gap that state laws fill — and some states (especially Texas) are even stricter than HIPAA.

Auxilium's approach: Build to HIPAA Security Rule standards anyway (AES-256 encryption, TLS 1.2+, SOC 2 Type II) as a design baseline that satisfies most state requirements.

Credit Repair Organizations Act (CROA) & TSR Debt Relief Rules
MEDIUM-HIGH

If Auxilium is classified as a debt relief service, the FTC's Telemarketing Sales Rule could prohibit charging fees upfront.

Key distinction: Auxilium must market itself as facilitating a federal administrative process, NOT as reducing or eliminating debt. Language matters!

AI Regulatory Sandboxes (AZ & UT)
OPPORTUNITY

Arizona and Utah have created regulatory sandboxes that allow legal tech companies to operate under reduced UPL restrictions. These could provide a safer testing ground for Auxilium's more automated features.

Legal Case Studies

Real cases that shape how healthcare dispute tech can — and can't — operate

For an 8-Year-Old:

These are stories about what happened when other companies tried to help people with their bills. Some got in trouble because they went too far. Learning from their mistakes helps Auxilium stay safe!

Florida Bar v. TIKD Services (2021) — The Company That Got Shut Down

+
UPL FLORIDA PRECEDENT-SETTING

What Happened:

TIKD was a tech company that helped people contest traffic tickets. Users uploaded their ticket, TIKD handled everything, and charged a fee. Sounds helpful, right?

The Problem:

The Florida Bar said TIKD was practicing law without a license. Even though TIKD argued it was just a technology platform, the court said filing documents with a legal body and exercising judgment about legal matters = practicing law.

The Result:

Permanently enjoined (shut down). The court didn't care that no consumers were harmed.

Lesson for Auxilium:

Auxilium must be the tool, not the lawyer. It populates forms with the patient's data. It tracks deadlines. But it never advises whether to file, predicts outcomes, or makes strategic decisions. The patient always decides.

FTC v. DoNotPay (2024) — The $193K Fine for "Robot Lawyer" Claims

+
FTC ENFORCEMENT FEDERAL AI MARKETING

What Happened:

DoNotPay marketed itself as the "world's first robot lawyer" and claimed its AI could replace human attorneys for legal tasks including dispute letters and legal filings.

The Problem:

The FTC found DoNotPay's claims were misleading. The AI wasn't actually capable of providing legal services equivalent to a human lawyer. The marketing overpromised what the technology could deliver.

The Result:

$193,000 fine and required to stop making misleading claims about AI capabilities.

Lesson for Auxilium:

Never claim AI can replace legal professionals. Auxilium is "assistive admin automation" — not a lawyer, not a legal advisor, not a decision-maker. Marketing language is legally dispositive (the exact words you use determine your legal risk).

Texas Medical Association v. HHS (TMA I, II, III) — The Battle Over IDR Rules

+
ONGOING 5TH CIRCUIT NSA FRAMEWORK

What's Happening:

The Texas Medical Association has filed multiple lawsuits challenging how CMS implemented the No Surprises Act's IDR process.

TMA II (Final, 2024):

The 5th Circuit eliminated the QPA (Qualifying Payment Amount) presumption in IDR. Arbitrators can no longer automatically default to the QPA — they must consider all factors equally.

TMA III (Pending):

Challenges how the QPA itself is calculated. Reheard en banc in September 2025. Decision pending.

Why It Matters for Auxilium:

While TMA litigation targets IDR (not PPDR directly), the regulatory instability affects the entire NSA framework. Auxilium must build with architectural flexibility to accommodate rule changes. The IDR Operations Proposed Rule from November 2023 remains unfinalized.

Key Stakeholders

Everyone who has a role in healthcare billing disputes

For an 8-Year-Old:

Imagine a school play. There are actors, directors, stage crew, and the audience. In healthcare billing, there are also different "characters" who each play a part. Let's meet them!

🧑

Patients (Uninsured & Self-Pay)

Kid version: The person who went to the doctor and got a bill that's too high.

Can file PPDR disputes if bill exceeds GFE by $400+. Protected from retaliation. 1 in 6 U.S. adults carry medical debt. 50% can't pay a $500 unexpected medical bill.

🏥

Healthcare Providers (Hospitals, Doctors, Facilities)

Kid version: The doctors and hospitals who send the bills.

Must provide GFEs. Must respond to PPDR disputes within 10 business days. Spend ~$40B annually on billing. 41% report >10% denial rates. Can offer settlements during PPDR.

🏛

CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services)

Kid version: The government team that makes the rules and makes sure everyone follows them.

Administers the PPDR process. Selects SDR entities. Publishes guidance (through FAQ Set 71). Enforces GFE requirements. Can impose $100/day penalties on non-compliant providers.

⚖

SDR Entities (Currently C2C Innovative Solutions)

Kid version: The referee who listens to both sides and makes the final call.

Selected by HHS to resolve PPDR disputes. Must complete conflict checks. Issues binding determinations within 30 business days. Applies the two-pronged test (medical necessity + unforeseen circumstances).

💻

Health Tech / RCM Companies (Auxilium's Lane)

Kid version: The smart helper that uses computers to make the whole process easier and faster.

67% of providers believe AI can improve claims, yet only 14% have adopted it. Must navigate UPL, privacy, and regulatory positioning. $5.13B denial management market (2024), growing to $8.93B by 2030.

🛡

Health Insurance Plans / Payers

Kid version: The insurance companies. They're involved in IDR (not PPDR), but their behavior creates the problem.

Denials rose 16% from 2018-2024. 20% of ACA Marketplace claims denied in 2023. May resist tools that make appeals easier. Auxilium approaches payers later in GTM strategy.

💼

Self-Insured Employers

Kid version: Big companies that pay for their workers' health bills directly instead of buying insurance.

63% of covered workers are in self-funded plans. They pay claims directly and feel the financial pain. Neutral-to-positive on dispute resolution tools. Key Auxilium customer segment.

📜

State Regulators & Legislators

Kid version: People in each state who make extra rules on top of the federal ones.

Create state privacy laws (TX TMRPA, WA MHMDA, CA CCPA). Define UPL in their jurisdictions. Some offer regulatory sandboxes (AZ, UT). State surprise billing laws govern fully insured plans.
