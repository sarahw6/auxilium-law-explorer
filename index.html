No Surprises Act (NSA)

Pub. L. 116-260 • Signed Dec 2020 • Effective Jan 1, 2022
TL;DR: You can't get ambushed by a medical bill you never agreed to. If you're uninsured, providers must give you a price estimate upfront, and you can fight back if the bill is $400+ over that estimate.
Imagine ordering a $5 burger and the restaurant charges you $50 because the cook wasn't "in your plan." This law says: NOPE. They have to tell you the real price first.
✅ What it does
  • Bans surprise bills for emergencies
  • Bans surprise bills from OON docs at in-network hospitals
  • Requires Good Faith Estimates for uninsured patients
  • Creates PPDR dispute process ($25 to file)
  • Creates IDR process for insurer-provider fights
⚠ Watch out
  • Only applies to care after Jan 1, 2022
  • PPDR is only for uninsured/self-pay patients
  • Need a GFE to file — no GFE = no dispute
  • 120-day filing deadline is ABSOLUTE
  • Co-provider GFE enforcement deferred indefinitely

Key sections: PHS Act §§2799B-6, 2799B-7 • 45 CFR §§149.610–149.620 • Amended PHS Act, ERISA, and Internal Revenue Code

HIPAA

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act • 1996
TL;DR: The godfather of health privacy. Tells hospitals and insurers how to protect your data. Plot twist: it probably does NOT apply to Auxilium — because Auxilium isn't a hospital or insurer. But Auxilium builds to HIPAA standards anyway, because why not be the safest kid on the block?
HIPAA is like a rule that says the school nurse can't tell your classmates you got a flu shot. But it only applies to the nurse and the doctor — not to your friend you told at lunch.
🏥 Who it covers

Covered entities (hospitals, doctors, insurers) and their business associates. When a patient voluntarily shares bills with a third party, HIPAA doesn't follow that data.

💡 The Auxilium twist

HIPAA likely doesn't apply — but this creates a GAP that state laws fill. Texas, Washington, and California each have laws that catch companies HIPAA misses. Auxilium builds to HIPAA Security Rule standards (AES-256, TLS 1.2+) as a baseline.

45 CFR Parts 160 & 164 • Privacy Rule, Security Rule, Breach Notification Rule • Penalties up to $1.5M/year per violation category

FTC Health Breach Notification Rule

16 CFR Part 318 • The law that ACTUALLY governs Auxilium's data
TL;DR: Since HIPAA probably doesn't cover Auxilium, THIS is the federal law that does. If you're a company that handles health data and you're not a hospital or insurer, the FTC is watching you. Get breached? You have 60 days to tell everyone.
If someone breaks into the box where you keep your health papers, this rule says you have to tell everyone whose papers got stolen — and you have to do it fast!
💰 Penalty

$50,120 per incident

Plus 60-day notification requirement to affected individuals, FTC, and (if 500+ people) media.

🔍 Why it matters

Covers "vendors of personal health records" — entities maintaining identifiable health info from multiple sources. That description fits Auxilium perfectly.

FTC Act Section 5

Unfair or Deceptive Acts or Practices • The "don't lie to your customers" law
TL;DR: Don't trick people. If you use AI, be honest about what it can and can't do. Tell patients they can file PPDR disputes themselves for $25 instead of paying you. Don't predict outcomes. Don't call yourself a "robot lawyer."
It's like the rule that says you can't put a picture of a huge pizza on the box if the actual pizza inside is tiny. You have to be honest!
📋 Auxilium must
  • Disclose ALL fees prominently
  • Tell patients they can self-file for $25
  • Never claim AI gives "legal advice"
  • Never predict dispute success rates
💥 Precedent

FTC fined DoNotPay $193,000 in 2024 for calling itself "the world's first robot lawyer." Marketing language is legally dispositive — the exact words you choose determine your legal risk.

Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL)

State-by-state doctrine • The #1 existential risk for legal tech
TL;DR: If your software crosses the line from "filling out a form" to "giving legal advice," you're practicing law without a license. TIKD got permanently shut down for this. DoNotPay got fined. The line between "tool" and "lawyer" is where legal tech companies live or die.
Imagine pretending to be a pilot and flying a real airplane. You'd get in BIG trouble even if the plane landed safely! That's what happens when a computer pretends to be a lawyer.
✅ Safe zone
  • Filling in forms with patient's data
  • Tracking deadlines and reminders
  • Collecting and organizing documents
  • Generating template-based letters
  • Showing CMS eligibility criteria
🛑 Danger zone
  • Advising whether to file
  • Predicting outcomes
  • Recommending strategies
  • Exercising legal judgment
  • Customizing arguments

Escape hatch: Arizona and Utah regulatory sandboxes allow legal tech companies to test features under reduced UPL restrictions.

ERISA

Employee Retirement Income Security Act • 1974 • The employer plan overlord
TL;DR: If your health insurance comes from your job, ERISA is the boss of it. ERISA preempts most state insurance laws for employer-sponsored plans, which is why the federal No Surprises Act matters so much — it's one of the few laws that reaches ERISA plans. 63% of covered workers are in self-funded ERISA plans.
When your parent's job gives them health insurance, a special federal rule is in charge instead of the state rules. It's like the principal overruling the teacher.
📊 Key stat

63%

of covered workers are in self-funded ERISA plans. These employers pay claims directly and feel the financial pain of denials.

🔗 NSA connection

The NSA amended ERISA to apply surprise billing protections to self-funded plans. For PPDR specifically, ERISA doesn't apply because PPDR serves uninsured/self-pay patients, not employer plan members.

Affordable Care Act (ACA)

Pub. L. 111-148 • 2010 • "Obamacare"
TL;DR: The ACA expanded insurance coverage to millions, created the Marketplace, and requires insurers to cover essential health benefits. But it didn't fix surprise billing — that took the NSA a decade later. 20% of ACA Marketplace claims were denied in 2023.
This is the law that said everyone should be able to get health insurance, even if they're already sick. It made a big online store where you can shop for insurance.
🎯 Relevant pieces
  • Essential health benefits requirement
  • Internal & external appeals rights
  • Marketplace plan standards
  • Preventive care coverage mandates
🤔 The gap the NSA fills

The ACA gave people insurance but didn't stop providers from sending surprise out-of-network bills. The NSA plugs that hole. Together they create the modern patient protection framework.

Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)

15 U.S.C. §1692 • 1977 • "Stop calling me at dinner"
TL;DR: Debt collectors can't harass you, lie to you, or use unfair tactics. During a PPDR dispute, providers CAN'T send your bill to collections at all — that's the NSA's anti-retaliation rule ($100/day penalty). The FDCPA adds another layer if they try.
If someone keeps calling your house to ask for money in a mean way, this law says they have to be polite and follow rules. And during a PPDR dispute, they can't call at all!
🛡 Protections
  • No calls before 8am or after 9pm
  • Must stop contact if you request in writing
  • Can't misrepresent amount owed
  • Can't threaten violence or criminal charges
  • Must verify debt within 5 days
💰 NSA bonus

The NSA goes further: during PPDR, providers must cease all collections, can't threaten collections, and can't charge late fees. Violation = $100/day federal penalty. Even after PPDR, reporting the disputed amount to credit bureaus may violate both the FDCPA and FCRA.

📈

Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

15 U.S.C. §1681 • 1970 • Your credit score's bodyguard
TL;DR: Credit bureaus must be accurate. Medical debt under $500 can no longer appear on credit reports (as of 2023). Disputed medical debt must be removed during investigation. Providers who report a bill that's under active PPDR dispute may be violating the FCRA.
Your credit score is like a report card for money. This law says nobody can put wrong things on it. If a hospital bill is being argued about, they can't put it on your money report card.
📊 Recent changes
  • Medical debt under $500 removed from reports (2023)
  • Paid medical debt removed from reports
  • 1-year waiting period before unpaid medical debt appears
  • CFPB proposed removing all medical debt from reports
🔗 Auxilium connection

Post-PPDR monitoring for unauthorized credit reporting is a key Auxilium feature. If a provider reports a bill that's under active dispute, Auxilium generates a dispute letter citing FCRA protections.

IRC Section 501(r)

Nonprofit Hospital Financial Assistance • ACA addition • 2010
TL;DR: If a hospital is nonprofit (and most big ones are), it MUST have a financial assistance policy and can't charge uninsured patients more than insured patients pay. If you can't afford a bill, the hospital is legally required to have a program to help you. Most people don't know this.
Big hospitals that don't pay taxes have to help people who can't afford their bills. It's like the school having a free lunch program — it has to exist, but you have to ask for it!
✅ Requirements
  • Written financial assistance policy
  • Can't charge uninsured more than insured
  • Must make reasonable effort to inform patients
  • No extraordinary collection actions before screening
🔗 Auxilium alt-path

When PPDR isn't available (no GFE, deadline passed, sub-$400 excess), Auxilium can screen patients for 501(r) financial assistance eligibility as an alternative pathway. This is pure information — no UPL risk.

CROA + TSR Debt Relief Rules

Credit Repair Organizations Act + FTC Telemarketing Sales Rule
TL;DR: If you help people reduce debt, you're a "debt relief service" and can't charge upfront fees. Auxilium's survival depends on NOT being classified as debt relief. The magic words: "We facilitate a federal administrative process" — not "We reduce your medical debt."
There are special rules for companies that help people who owe money. Auxilium has to be careful to say it helps you fill out government forms, NOT that it makes your bills go away.
⚠ If classified as debt relief

TSR 16 CFR §310.4(a)(5)(i) could prohibit advance fees. CROA could require registration. This would fundamentally break Auxilium's business model.

✅ How to avoid

Market as facilitating a federal administrative filing, not reducing debt. Never use words like "eliminate," "reduce," or "settle" medical debt in marketing materials. Language is legally dispositive.

Texas is the scariest: any company that touches health data for profit faces HIPAA-equivalent rules. Washington requires separate opt-in consent for health data. TL;DR: Doctors (TMA) are suing the government (HHS) over how the No Surprises Act is implemented. TMA II eliminated the QPA presumption. TMA III (pending) challenges how the QPA is calculated. The IDR Operations Proposed Rule remains unfinalized. This litigation creates regulatory instability that Auxilium must architect around.

The No Surprises Act — Deep Dive

Signed December 27, 2020 • Effective January 1, 2022 • Pub. L. 116-260

For an 8-Year-Old:

Imagine you go to a restaurant. The menu says a burger is $5. But when the check comes, they charge you $50 because the cook wasn't "in your plan." The No Surprises Act says: restaurants (hospitals) have to tell you the real price AND can't surprise you with a huge bill from a cook (doctor) you didn't pick.

Three Big Protections

1. No Surprise Bills

Ban on surprise billing for emergency services and non-emergency services at in-network facilities.

2. Cost Protection for Uninsured

If you don't have insurance, you get a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) before your visit, and can dispute bills $400+ over the estimate.

3. Price Transparency

Hospitals and doctors must be upfront about costs. No hiding fees or making it hard to find prices.

Federal IDR vs. PPDR

Federal IDR

Between providers and health plans for insured patients. ~1.19M disputes in H1 2025. Provider win rate: ~88%.

PPDR (Auxilium's Focus)

Between patients and providers for uninsured/self-pay patients. Patient-favorable structure. 45 CFR §149.620.

Five Requirements to File a PPDR Dispute

1
Uninsured or Self-Pay

No group plan, individual coverage, Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, VA, or IHS.

2
Care After January 1, 2022

Services must have been provided on or after the NSA effective date.

3
Good Faith Estimate Received

You must have received a GFE. No GFE = no PPDR (file a CMS complaint instead).

4
$400+ Over Estimate

Billed charges exceed GFE by $400+ per provider. Calculated independently, NOT aggregated.

5
Within 120 Days

Must file within 120 calendar days of receiving the initial bill.

Anti-Retaliation Protection

Providers cannot send disputed bills to collections, threaten collections, or charge late fees during PPDR. Penalty: $100/day per violation.

PPDR Process Flow

Step-by-step: How a patient dispute actually works

For an 8-Year-Old:

It's like returning something at a store. You fill out a form, pay a tiny fee ($25), a fair helper looks at both sides, and then decides what you really owe. If you win, you even get your $25 back!

Day 0

1. File the Dispute

Submit form CMS-10791 via the Federal IDR Portal, electronically, or by mail to C2C Innovative Solutions.

Upon Request

2. Pay $25 Admin Fee

Pay via credit card, PayPal, Venmo, or money order. If you win, this $25 gets deducted from what you owe.

Days 1-5

3. SDR Entity Assigned

HHS selects a neutral SDR entity (currently C2C Innovative Solutions). They check eligibility with a 21-business-day cure window.

+3 Business Days

4. Conflict Check

SDR entity checks for conflicts of interest. If conflicted, HHS reassigns.

10 Business Days

5. Provider Responds

Provider submits "credible information" showing charges reflect medically necessary service based on unforeseen circumstances.

During Step 5

6. Settlement Window

Provider may offer settlement. Must reduce patient liability by at least $12.50 (50% of $25 fee).

30 Business Days

7. Final Decision

Binding determination. No appeal. If provider fails: you pay GFE amount (GFE items) or $0 (non-GFE items).

After the Decision

If Patient Wins

  • Provider adjusts bill within 30 days
  • $25 fee deducted from amount owed
  • Monitor for unauthorized credit reporting

If Provider Wins

  • Patient owes lesser of billed charge or median rate
  • $25 fee NOT refunded
  • No appeal — but can negotiate directly

✊ Police Brutality & Medical Billing

If a police officer hurts someone and that person goes to the hospital, they might get a huge bill even though it wasn't their fault. That's not fair, and there are laws and people who can help.

Survivors of police violence face a cruel double burden: physical and emotional trauma compounded by medical bills they never asked for. Here's what to know:

  • Emergency rooms MUST treat you regardless of ability to pay — the Emergency Medical Treatment & Labor Act (EMTALA) requires hospitals to stabilize anyone in an emergency, regardless of insurance or immigration status.
  • 42 U.S.C. §1983 civil rights claims — Victims of police brutality can sue for damages including medical expenses. Consult a civil rights attorney (many work on contingency, meaning no upfront cost).
  • State Crime Victim Compensation Programs — Every state has a victim compensation fund that can cover medical bills, mental health treatment, lost wages, and funeral costs. Typically $10,000–$100,000+ available. Apply through your state's Attorney General or public safety office.
  • If you're uninsured, the hospital MUST provide a Good Faith Estimate if you identify as self-pay, and PPDR may apply if the bill exceeds the GFE by $400+.
  • Nonprofit hospitals (501(r)) must offer financial assistance programs. Ask for a financial counselor before accepting any payment plan.
  • Document everything — photos, police reports, witness contacts, medical records. This evidence supports both your legal case and any billing disputes.

🏥 Uninsured & Facing a Health Emergency

If someone gets really sick but doesn't have insurance, they can still go to the emergency room. The hospital has to help them. And there are programs to help pay the bill afterward.

If you're uninsured and need emergency care, you have more rights than you think:

  • EMTALA guarantees emergency treatment — No hospital can turn you away from the ER based on ability to pay. This is federal law.
  • Ask for a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) — Under the No Surprises Act, tell the hospital you are self-pay. They MUST give you a written cost estimate. If the final bill exceeds it by $400+, you can file a PPDR dispute for just $25.
  • 501(r) financial assistance — If the hospital is nonprofit (most major hospitals are), they are legally required to have a financial assistance policy. Ask for a charity care application. Many hospitals will reduce or forgive bills entirely for low-income patients.
  • Negotiate before you pay — Request an itemized bill. Look for errors (duplicate charges, wrong codes). Hospitals often accept 20–60% less than the initial bill through negotiation.
  • Medicaid may cover retroactively — In many states, Medicaid can cover bills from up to 3 months before your application date. Apply even after receiving care.
  • Payment plans — Hospitals must offer interest-free payment plans before sending bills to collections. Some states require 120+ days before any collection activity.

🚗 Car Accident — Uninsured or Underinsured

If someone gets in a car crash and doesn't have insurance, they still need to go to the hospital. There are ways to handle the bills, and the person who caused the crash may have to pay.

Car accidents create complex billing situations, especially for the uninsured:

  • Emergency treatment cannot be refused — EMTALA applies. Go to the ER. Your life and health come first.
  • If the other driver is at fault, their auto liability insurance should cover your medical bills. File a claim with their insurer. If they're uninsured, your own Uninsured Motorist (UM) coverage may apply.
  • Personal Injury Protection (PIP) — In "no-fault" states (FL, MI, NJ, NY, PA, and others), your own auto policy covers your medical bills regardless of fault. Check your policy.
  • Medical Payments Coverage (MedPay) — If you have this on your auto policy, it covers medical bills regardless of fault, up to your policy limit.
  • If you're completely uninsured (no health or auto insurance):
    • Tell the hospital you're self-pay to trigger GFE/No Surprises Act protections
    • Apply for hospital charity care (501(r))
    • Apply for Medicaid (may cover retroactively)
    • If the other driver is at fault, consult a personal injury attorney (most work on contingency)
  • Don't sign anything from the at-fault driver's insurance without understanding your rights. Early settlement offers are often far below what you deserve.

💜 Losing Someone — Grief, Bills & What to Know

When someone we love dies, it hurts more than anything. And sometimes bills arrive after they're gone. It's okay to ask for help with both the sadness and the paperwork.

Losing someone is devastating. Here's what you need to know about the practical side, so you can focus on healing:

  • You are generally NOT responsible for a deceased family member's medical debt — Unless you co-signed, were a spouse in a community property state, or are the estate executor. Debt collectors cannot legally tell you otherwise.
  • Beware of debt collector tactics — Some collectors pressure grieving family members into paying debts they don't owe. Know your rights under the FDCPA. You can demand written verification.
  • The estate pays first — Medical debts are paid from the deceased person's estate (their assets). If the estate has insufficient assets, unsecured medical debt is often discharged.
  • Community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI) — Surviving spouses may be liable for medical debt incurred during the marriage. Consult a probate attorney.
  • Hospital financial assistance still applies — Apply for charity care or financial assistance on the deceased's bills. Many hospitals will reduce or forgive post-mortem bills.
  • State crime victim compensation — If the death resulted from a crime (including police violence, DUI, assault), compensation can cover funeral costs ($5,000–$25,000+ depending on the state).

💭 Anxiety & Overwhelm from Medical Bills

Sometimes getting a big scary bill makes people feel so worried they can't sleep or think straight. That's called anxiety, and it's very normal. There are people who can help you feel better AND deal with the bill.

Medical debt is a leading cause of anxiety, depression, and hopelessness in America. You are not alone, and your feelings are valid:

  • 50% of Americans cannot afford a $500 unexpected medical bill (KFF, 2025). If you feel overwhelmed, you are in the majority.
  • Medical debt anxiety is a recognized health impact — Studies show it contributes to depression, delayed care, and worsened health outcomes. Your mental health matters.
  • You don't have to handle this alone — Patient advocates, financial counselors (often free at hospitals), and nonprofit organizations can help you navigate bills.
  • Anxiety management resources:
    • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — Call or text 988, even if you're not suicidal. They help with anxiety and emotional distress too.
    • Crisis Text Line — Text HOME to 741741 for anxiety support
    • NAMI (nami.org) — Free peer support groups, education, and advocacy
    • Open Path Collective (openpathcollective.org) — Affordable therapy, $30-$80/session, no insurance needed
    • 7 Cups (7cups.com) — Free online emotional support chat, 24/7
  • Practical first steps: Don't ignore the bill — but don't panic either. Request an itemized bill. Ask for financial assistance. Set up a payment plan. Every hospital is required to work with you before sending to collections.

Medical debt contributes to feelings of hopelessness, but no bill is worth your life. Here's what you need to hear:

  • Medical debt can be discharged in bankruptcy — it's the most common factor in personal bankruptcy filings, and there is life after bankruptcy. It's a legal tool, not a moral failing.
  • Debt collectors cannot take everything — Federal and state exemption laws protect your home (in most states), retirement accounts, Social Security, disability benefits, and essential personal property.
  • Most medical debt falls off credit reports — Under recent changes, medical debt under $500 is no longer reported. Paid medical debt is removed. The financial impact is increasingly limited.
  • Free legal help exists — Legal Aid societies provide free representation for low-income individuals facing debt issues. Visit lawhelp.org to find help near you.
  • You deserve care, not punishment — Getting sick or injured is not a character flaw. The system that created $194 billion in medical debt is broken — you are not.
